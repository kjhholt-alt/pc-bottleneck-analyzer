# Is My PC Thermal Throttling? How to Detect and Fix Overheating in 2026

Your benchmark scores are lower than they should be. Your game starts smooth but tanks after 20 minutes. Your fans sound like a jet engine. You've checked your GPU utilization, your CPU usage, your RAM — everything looks fine on paper. But something is quietly strangling your performance.

That something is probably **thermal throttling**.

Thermal throttling is one of the most common — and most overlooked — performance bottlenecks in modern PCs. It happens when your CPU or GPU gets so hot that it automatically reduces its clock speed to prevent damage. The result? You lose 10–40% of your hardware's performance potential, and most people never realize it's happening.

This guide will show you exactly how to detect thermal throttling, understand why it happens, and fix it — whether you're dealing with a toasty laptop or a desktop that's slowly cooking itself.

## What Is Thermal Throttling, Exactly?

Every processor — CPU and GPU alike — has a maximum safe operating temperature called **T-junction** (Tj Max) for CPUs or **GPU Temperature Limit** for graphics cards. When your chip approaches this limit, it does one of two things:

1. **Reduces clock speed** — The chip lowers its operating frequency, directly reducing performance.
2. **Reduces voltage** — Lower voltage means less heat, but also less performance headroom.

Modern processors are *designed* to do this. It's a safety mechanism, not a defect. But it means your expensive hardware is only delivering a fraction of the performance you paid for.

Here's what thermal throttling looks like in practice:

| Chip | Rated Boost Clock | Throttled Clock | Performance Loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| Intel Core i7-14700K | 5.6 GHz | 4.2 GHz | ~25% |
| AMD Ryzen 7 9800X3D | 5.2 GHz | 3.8 GHz | ~27% |
| NVIDIA RTX 5070 Ti | 2.62 GHz | 2.1 GHz | ~20% |
| AMD RX 9070 XT | 2.85 GHz | 2.3 GHz | ~19% |

Those aren't worst-case scenarios — those are realistic numbers we see regularly from users running stock coolers or poorly ventilated cases.

## How to Tell If Your PC Is Thermal Throttling

### Step 1: Monitor Temperatures Under Load

The single best tool for this is **HWiNFO64** (free). Here's exactly what to do:

1. Download and open HWiNFO64 in "Sensors-only" mode.
2. Find your **CPU Package** or **CPU (Tctl/Tdie)** temperature sensor.
3. Find your **GPU Temperature** sensor.
4. Start a demanding workload — a game, Cinebench, or Blender render.
5. Watch the **Maximum** column over 10–15 minutes.

**Critical thresholds:**

- **Intel CPUs**: Tj Max is typically 100°C. Throttling begins around 95–100°C.
- **AMD CPUs**: Tj Max is typically 95°C (Ryzen 7000/9000). Throttling begins around 90–95°C.
- **NVIDIA GPUs**: Default thermal limit is 83°C for most RTX cards. Power limit throttling often starts before thermal throttling.
- **AMD GPUs**: Junction temperature limit is typically 110°C, but edge temperature throttling starts around 90°C.

If your CPU is hitting 95°C+ or your GPU is hitting 83°C+ under sustained load, you are almost certainly thermal throttling.

### Step 2: Look for Clock Speed Drops

Temperature alone doesn't confirm throttling — you need to see clock speeds dropping *because of* temperature. In HWiNFO64:

1. Watch **CPU Clock** (or individual core clocks) alongside temperature.
2. Look for a pattern: temperature rises → clock speed drops → temperature stabilizes at a lower clock.
3. Check the **Thermal Throttling** flag in HWiNFO64 — if it shows "Yes" at any point, that's definitive proof.

For GPUs, **MSI Afterburner** with the on-screen display (OSD) is excellent. Enable GPU Temperature, GPU Clock, and GPU Power overlays. If you see the clock gradually stepping down over time while temperature is at or near the limit, that's throttling.

### Step 3: Run a Time-Based Benchmark

One of the clearest ways to catch thermal throttling is to run a sustained benchmark and compare early results to late results:

- **Cinebench R24** (Multi-Core, 10-minute loop): If your score drops by more than 5% from the first run to the third consecutive run, thermal throttling is likely.
- **3DMark Time Spy Stress Test**: Runs the GPU benchmark 20 times. A stability rating below 95% often indicates thermal throttling.
- **AIDA64 Stability Test**: Monitors everything over time and flags thermal events.

## Why Is My PC Overheating? The 7 Most Common Causes

### 1. Dried or Poorly Applied Thermal Paste

Thermal paste degrades over time. After 2–3 years, it can dry out and lose conductivity. This is the **#1 cause** of gradual performance degradation in systems that "used to run fine."

**Fix:** Reapply thermal paste. Use a quality compound like <AffiliateLink name="Noctua NT-H2" /> or <AffiliateLink name="Thermal Grizzly Kryonaut" />. Clean the old paste with 90%+ isopropyl alcohol and a lint-free cloth. Apply a small pea-sized dot in the center of the CPU.

### 2. Inadequate CPU Cooler

Modern CPUs are power-hungry. Intel's 14th-gen K-series chips can pull 250W+ under sustained load. AMD's Ryzen 9000 series chips are more efficient but still need serious cooling for maximum boost clocks.

The stock cooler that came with your CPU (if it even included one) is designed for base clock operation, not sustained boost. If you're running an unlocked chip on the stock cooler, you're almost guaranteed to throttle.

- **Mid-range**: <AffiliateLink name="Noctua NH-D15" /> (~$110) — the gold standard for air cooling
- **High-end**: <AffiliateLink name="Arctic Liquid Freezer III 360" /> (~$100) — best value AIO on the market

### 3. Poor Case Airflow

A beautiful case with a solid glass front panel and two tiny intake fans is a thermal trap. Your components are generating hundreds of watts of heat — that heat needs somewhere to go.

**Signs of poor airflow:**
- Both CPU and GPU run hot (not just one)
- Temperatures are fine on an open bench but bad inside the case
- The inside of the case feels like an oven when you open the side panel

**Fix:** Ensure you have a front-to-back or bottom-to-top airflow path. Aim for at least 2–3 intake fans and 1–2 exhaust fans. Consider a mesh-front case like the <AffiliateLink name="Fractal Design North" /> or <AffiliateLink name="Lian Li Lancool III" /> if your current case is restricting airflow.

### 4. Dust Buildup

Dust is an insulator. A 3mm layer of dust on your heatsink fins can raise temperatures by 10–15°C. If you haven't cleaned your PC in the last 6 months, this is a free fix.

**Fix:** Use compressed air to blow out dust from heatsink fins, fan blades, and case filters. Do this outside or in a well-ventilated area. Clean dust filters monthly.

### 5. Aggressive Power Limits (Intel Specific)

Intel's 13th and 14th-gen desktop chips are notorious for running hot because many motherboard manufacturers ship with unlimited power limits by default. Your i7-14700K might be pulling 253W sustained when Intel's recommended PL2 is 253W for short bursts only.

**Fix:** Enter BIOS and enforce Intel's recommended power limits (PBP/MTP). On most boards, look for "Intel Default Settings" or manually set PL1/PL2. You'll lose ~3–5% multi-core performance but gain dramatically better thermals and actually *improve* sustained performance by avoiding throttling.

### 6. GPU Thermal Pad Degradation

GPU thermal pads (on VRAM and VRMs) can degrade or shift over time, especially in cards that run hot. This is particularly common in RTX 30-series cards that have been running for 3+ years.

**Fix:** Replacing GPU thermal pads is more advanced but doable. Use quality pads like <AffiliateLink name="Gelid GP-Ultimate" /> at the correct thickness for your card model. Check teardown guides specific to your GPU before attempting.

### 7. Ambient Temperature

This one is obvious but often ignored. A room at 30°C (86°F) vs. 20°C (68°F) means your components start 10°C hotter. In summer months, what was a stable overclock can become a throttling mess.

**Fix:** Air conditioning, better room ventilation, or adjusting fan curves seasonally. Some users create summer and winter fan curve profiles.

## How to Fix Thermal Throttling: Step-by-Step Priority

If you've confirmed thermal throttling, work through these fixes in order from easiest to most involved:

### Level 1: Free Fixes (Do These First)
1. **Clean dust** from all heatsinks, fans, and filters.
2. **Optimize fan curves** — Use your motherboard's fan software or BIOS to create a more aggressive curve. Many default curves prioritize silence over cooling.
3. **Improve cable management** — Bunched cables blocking airflow paths can raise temps by 3–5°C.
4. **Check case fan orientation** — Make sure fans aren't fighting each other. Front/bottom = intake, rear/top = exhaust.
5. **Enforce Intel default power limits** if running an Intel K-series chip.

### Level 2: Low-Cost Fixes ($10–50)
1. **Reapply thermal paste** on CPU (~$10 for quality paste).
2. **Add case fans** if you have empty mounts. Quality 120mm fans are $10–15 each.
3. **Remove side panel** temporarily to confirm airflow is the issue — if temps drop significantly, your case is the problem.

### Level 3: Moderate Investment ($50–150)
1. **Upgrade CPU cooler** to a proper tower or AIO.
2. **Switch to a mesh-front case** if your current case has poor airflow.
3. **Undervolt your GPU** using MSI Afterburner — this is free and can reduce GPU temps by 10–15°C with minimal performance loss (often 0–2%).

## GPU Undervolting: The Best-Kept Secret for Thermal Throttling

This deserves its own section because it's genuinely transformative and completely free.

GPU undervolting reduces the voltage supplied to your GPU at each clock speed, lowering power consumption and heat output without meaningfully reducing performance. Here's the quick version:

1. Open **MSI Afterburner** and press Ctrl+F to open the voltage/frequency curve.
2. Find the voltage your GPU currently runs at under load (typically 1.0–1.1V).
3. Set a flat curve at a lower voltage (try 0.9V to start) at your desired clock speed.
4. Test with a benchmark. If stable, try going lower. If it crashes, bump up 25mV.

Many users report **10–20°C temperature drops** with only a 1–3% performance loss. Some cards actually perform *better* undervolted because they stop thermal throttling and maintain higher sustained clocks.

## When Thermal Throttling Means It's Upgrade Time

Sometimes the fix isn't better cooling — it's recognizing that your hardware has hit its thermal wall. This is especially true for:

- **Laptops** with thin chassis that can't dissipate heat effectively
- **Small Form Factor (SFF) builds** with limited cooler clearance
- **Aging systems** where paste, pads, and fans have all degraded

If you've exhausted all cooling fixes and your system still throttles, consider whether a more power-efficient newer-gen CPU might actually give you better real-world performance despite similar benchmark scores. AMD's Ryzen 9000 series is remarkably efficient compared to Intel's 14th-gen, often delivering similar gaming performance at 60–70% of the power draw.

## Thermal Throttling FAQ

**Is some thermal throttling normal?**
Brief, minor throttling during extreme loads (e.g., first few seconds of a heavy benchmark) is normal for many CPUs, especially on Intel platforms. It's sustained throttling that kills performance.

**Can thermal throttling damage my PC?**
No — that's the whole point. Throttling is a safety mechanism. However, running at high temperatures consistently can reduce component lifespan over years.

**Do I need liquid cooling?**
Not necessarily. High-end air coolers like the <AffiliateLink name="Noctua NH-D15" /> match or beat many 240mm AIOs. Liquid cooling is mainly necessary for high-TDP chips (200W+) or SFF builds with limited cooler height clearance.

**My laptop thermal throttles — what can I do?**
Laptop cooling is limited by design. Your best options: use a laptop cooling pad, undervolt the CPU/GPU (use ThrottleStop for Intel, Ryzen Controller for AMD), reduce power limits in BIOS if available, and ensure vents aren't blocked.
