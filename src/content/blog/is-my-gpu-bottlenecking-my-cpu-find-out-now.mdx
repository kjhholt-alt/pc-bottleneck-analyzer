# Is My GPU Bottlenecking My CPU? Here's How to Find Out (and Fix It)

You're mid-game, frames are tanking, and you're staring at your Task Manager wondering what's going on. Your CPU looks busy, your GPU looks… less busy. Or maybe it's the other way around. Either way, you're asking the right question: **is my GPU bottlenecking my CPU?**

The short answer: probably yes — but maybe not in the way you think. Let's break it down properly.

---

## TL;DR — Key Takeaways

- A **GPU bottleneck** means your GPU can't keep up with the frames your CPU wants to push — common at high resolutions
- A **CPU bottleneck** means your CPU can't feed frames fast enough to your GPU — common at 1080p with weak CPUs
- You can diagnose bottlenecks using in-game overlays, MSI Afterburner, or our [free bottleneck analyzer tool](/)
- Most mid-range builds in 2026 are GPU-limited at 1440p and above — that's actually *normal*
- Upgrading your GPU, RAM speed, or dropping to a higher resolution can all reduce bottlenecks

## What Does "GPU Bottlenecking CPU" Actually Mean?

Before we go any further, let's clear up a common misconception. When people ask "is my GPU bottlenecking my CPU?", they usually mean one of two things:

1. **GPU bottleneck**: Your GPU is the limiting factor — it's maxed out and holding back your potential frame rate
2. **CPU bottleneck**: Your CPU is the limiting factor — your GPU is sitting idle waiting for draw calls and game logic from the processor

Neither is inherently *bad*. In fact, **a slight GPU bottleneck is the ideal scenario for gaming** — it means you're getting maximum value out of your CPU. The goal is to have your GPU running at ~95–99% utilization while your CPU stays healthy below 80–85%.

Think of it like a pipeline. Your CPU fills the pipe with game data, and your GPU drains it by rendering frames. If the GPU drains slower than the CPU fills, you get a GPU bottleneck. If the CPU can't fill fast enough, you get a CPU bottleneck — and that's where you really start losing frames in busy, complex scenes.

## Signs Your GPU Is Bottlenecking Your CPU

Here's what to look for if you suspect a GPU bottleneck:

### Your GPU Usage Is Stuck at 99%

Open MSI Afterburner or check the GPU tab in Task Manager. If your GPU is pegged at 99% while your CPU is sitting comfortably at 40–60%, your GPU is the bottleneck. This is *completely normal* at 1440p and 4K — and it means your CPU isn't being wasted.

### Framerates Improve When You Lower Resolution or Settings

Drop from 1440p to 1080p, or turn down your shadow quality. If your FPS jumps significantly, that's your GPU breathing a sigh of relief. The CPU didn't change, but you gave the GPU less work to do.

### Low 1% / 0.1% Lows Without Consistent Drops

A GPU bottleneck tends to produce a pretty steady framerate — just lower than you'd like. If your average FPS is consistent but just not high enough, your GPU is working as hard as it can. Contrast this with a CPU bottleneck, which usually causes wild framerate swings and bad 1% lows.

## Signs Your CPU Is Bottlenecking Your GPU

This is the scenario most people are actually worried about — and it's genuinely worth fixing.

### Your GPU Usage Is Low (Below 80%) While CPU Is Maxed

If your GPU is sitting at 50–70% while one or more CPU cores are pinned at 100%, you've got a classic CPU bottleneck. Your GPU is literally waiting around with nothing to render because the CPU can't send frames fast enough.

### Bad 1% Lows and Frame Time Spikes

CPU bottlenecks are brutal for smoothness. You might average 90 FPS but have 1% lows of 40 FPS — that stuttery, jarring feel is almost always CPU-related. Games like *Cyberpunk 2077*, *Microsoft Flight Simulator 2024*, and *Star Citizen* are notoriously CPU-hungry and expose this fast.

### Worse Performance at Lower Resolutions

This one's counterintuitive. If dropping from 1440p to 1080p barely improves your FPS, that's actually a sign of a CPU bottleneck. At lower resolutions, the GPU finishes frames faster and spends even *more* time waiting on the CPU.

## How to Fix a CPU Bottleneck

A CPU bottleneck is more annoying to fix, but here's the path forward:

### 1. Enable XMP/EXPO for Your RAM (Free)

This is the most overlooked free fix on r/pcgaming. If your RAM isn't running at its rated speed (check in BIOS or CPU-Z), enable XMP (Intel) or EXPO (AMD). Running DDR4 at 2133MHz instead of 3600MHz can cost you 10–15% CPU gaming performance. Seriously — do this first.

### 2. Close Background Apps

Browser tabs, Discord video, streaming software — these eat CPU cores. For competitive gaming especially, a lean background is worth 5–10% more CPU headroom.

## What's a "Good" Bottleneck Percentage?

We get this question a lot. Here's a simple guide:

| GPU Usage | CPU Usage | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 95–99% | 50–75% | ✅ Ideal — GPU-limited, healthy CPU headroom |
| 85–94% | 75–85% | ✅ Good — balanced pairing |
| 70–84% | 85–95% | ⚠️ Mild CPU bottleneck — worth monitoring |
| Below 70% | 95–100% | ❌ Significant CPU bottleneck — upgrade CPU or RAM |
| 99% | Below 50% | 🔄 Heavy GPU bottleneck — upgrade GPU or lower resolution |

Remember, these are guidelines. Different games stress different components. A game like *Baldur's Gate 3* hammers the CPU; a title like *Cyberpunk 2077* with path tracing is almost entirely GPU-bound.

## Does RAM Affect GPU Bottleneck?

## FAQ: Is My GPU Bottlenecking My CPU?

### How do I check if my GPU is bottlenecking my CPU?

The easiest method is MSI Afterburner with RivaTuner — enable the overlay, look at GPU% and CPU% while gaming. If GPU is near 100% and CPU is under 80%, you have a GPU bottleneck (which is normal and healthy). If CPU is over 90% and GPU is under 75%, you have a CPU bottleneck.

Alternatively, use our [free bottleneck analyzer](/) to get an instant score for your exact CPU + GPU combo.

### Is a GPU bottleneck bad?

Not really. A GPU bottleneck just means your GPU is the performance ceiling, which is the intended design for gaming PCs. It ensures your CPU isn't wasted. The only time it's "bad" is when your GPU is too weak for your target resolution and settings, resulting in unacceptably low FPS.

### What percentage GPU usage is considered a bottleneck?

If your GPU is consistently above 95%, it's the bottleneck — and that's fine. If it's below 80% while your CPU is struggling, that's a CPU bottleneck worth addressing. The sweet spot is GPU at 95–99%, CPU at 60–80%.

### Can upgrading RAM fix a GPU bottleneck?

Indirectly, yes. Faster RAM reduces CPU processing time per frame, which means your GPU gets data faster and stays more consistently loaded. This is especially impactful on AMD Ryzen platforms. Enable XMP/EXPO first — it costs nothing and can meaningfully improve GPU utilization.

### Should I upgrade my CPU or GPU first?

Run our [free bottleneck analyzer](/) to find out which one is limiting your system. As a general rule: if you're gaming at 1440p or 4K, upgrade the GPU first. If you're on an older quad-core or gaming at 1080p competitively, the CPU might be the priority. Check out our post on [GPU bottlenecking your CPU](/blog/gpu-bottlenecking-cpu) for a deeper dive.
